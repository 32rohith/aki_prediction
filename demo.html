Early fusion via concatenation → MLP neural network (256→128→64). Combines both modalities for the best performance.

🚫 Anti-Leakage

Only features from the first 24 hours are used for prediction. AKI labels are computed only from data after 24h. Patient-level splitting prevents the same patient from appearing in both train and test sets.

📋 KDIGO Standard

AKI is defined using the international gold standard: 0.3 mg/dL creatinine rise in 48h OR 1.5× baseline in 7 days. Baseline creatinine uses same-admission pre-ICU values.

🔁 Reproducibility

All random operations use seed = 42. Phase completion markers ensure dependent stages don't run before prerequisites. All hyperparameters are saved as JSON files.

📊 Exploratory Data Analysis

Understanding the raw MIMIC-IV data before building any models. Click any image to zoom in.

Patient Age Distribution
Most ICU patients are 60–80 years old. The distribution is left-skewed, reflecting that ICU admissions are more common in elderly patients. AKI risk increases with age due to declining kidney reserve.
Gender Distribution
There's a slight male predominance in ICU admissions, consistent with published epidemiological literature. Gender is included as a binary feature in our model.
ICU Stay Duration (hours)
Most stays are 2–5 days. We filter out stays < 24 hours since we need at least 24h of data for both features and AKI evaluation. The long tail represents critically ill patients.
ICU Stays Per Patient
Most patients have 1–2 ICU stays, but some have 5+. This is why we use patient-level splitting — without it, the same patient's multiple stays could appear in both train and test sets, inflating results.
Raw Creatinine Value Distribution
Creatinine is the primary biomarker for kidney function. Normal range is 0.6–1.2 mg/dL. The right tail (values > 3) indicates patients with severe kidney impairment — these are the AKI cases we're trying to predict.
Lab Test Coverage Across ICU Stays
Not all labs are measured for every patient. We select only labs with ≥30% coverage. Common labs (BUN, sodium, potassium) are measured in nearly all patients. Rare labs are excluded because too much imputation introduces noise.
Lab Measurements Per ICU Stay
Each stay has a variable number of lab measurements. Since ML models need fixed-length inputs, we aggregate each lab into 6 statistics: mean, min, max, std, first, last values within the first 24 hours.
Feature Missingness Rates
Some features are heavily missing (60%+). We create binary missingness indicators (1=missing, 0=present) as additional features — the pattern of what's missing can itself be clinically informative. Missing values are imputed with training-set medians.

🏥 Cohort & AKI Analysis

After labeling with KDIGO criteria, we analyze the characteristics of AKI vs non-AKI patients.

AKI Prevalence in Cohort
About 21% of ICU stays develop AKI. This is consistent with clinical literature (10–30% for ICU populations). The class imbalance means we must use class_weight='balanced' in our models and track AUPRC (not just accuracy).
Baseline Creatinine: AKI vs Non-AKI
AKI patients have higher baseline creatinine on average. This makes clinical sense — patients with already-impaired kidneys are more vulnerable to further injury. This confirms our labeling logic is clinically valid.
Age Distribution: AKI vs Non-AKI
AKI patients tend to be slightly older, but the distributions overlap significantly. Age alone is not a strong predictor of AKI, but it contributes as part of the multivariate model.
Maximum Creatinine Increase (AKI cases)
Among AKI-positive patients, most show creatinine increases of 0.3–1.0 mg/dL. The 0.3 mg/dL threshold (KDIGO) is visible in the left edge. Some patients have dramatic 5+ mg/dL increases indicating severe AKI.
Feature Correlation Heatmap
Shows relationships between features. BUN and creatinine are strongly correlated (both reflect kidney function). High correlations between related labs (e.g., sodium/chloride) are expected. This helps identify redundant features and validates clinical relationships.

📈 Comprehensive Evaluation

All models evaluated on the held-out test set (11,170 patients never seen during training).

ROC Curves — All Models
ROC-AUC measures discrimination ability — can the model separate AKI from non-AKI? The structured and fusion models are closely clustered near 0.82 (excellent), while text-only models are at the diagonal (random). The closer to the top-left corner, the better.
Precision-Recall Curves — All Models
PR-AUC is more informative for imbalanced data (21% AKI). The fusion model achieves the best AUPRC of 0.589. Even with good discrimination, achieving high precision and recall simultaneously is challenging due to the 79/21 class split.
Calibration Curves (10-bin Reliability Diagram)
Calibration measures prediction trustworthiness — when the model says "70% risk," does 70% of those patients truly develop AKI? The diagonal line is perfect calibration. The MLP Fusion is best calibrated (Brier = 0.124), meaning clinicians can trust its probability estimates.
AUROC Comparison Bar Chart
Side-by-side comparison: Structured models (~0.815), Fusion MLP (~0.818), Text-only (~0.51). The small gap between structured and fusion shows that text adds marginal value in a 24h-restricted setting. In a less temporally constrained setting, the gap would likely be larger.
AUPRC Comparison Bar Chart
Fusion MLP has the highest AUPRC (0.589), indicating it finds more true AKI cases with fewer false alarms. This is the most clinically relevant metric since AKI is a minority class — we care about catching real AKI without crying wolf.
Precision / Recall / F1 Grouped Bar Chart
The tradeoff is visible: Fusion MLP has highest precision (0.70) but lower recall (0.35), while LR has higher recall (0.70) but lower precision (0.47). In clinical practice, the choice depends on whether you prefer fewer missed cases (recall) or fewer false alarms (precision).

💡 Key Insights

Text masking barely matters (0.26% drop) — The model gracefully falls back to structured features. This is ideal for deployment since most patients lack early notes.

Structured masking is catastrophic (40% drop) — Lab values are the dominant signal. Without them, the model is no better than random.

Clinical implication: The model is safe to deploy even when notes aren't yet available — it won't produce garbage predictions.
